# Hitting ~20 tok/s of Usable Elixir on a 9×P106-100 sm_61 Pascal Rig: A Throughput Decision Report

## TL;DR
- **You have likely already won the throughput fight per card.** A P106-100 generates a measured ~30 tok/s on a 7B Q4_0 model and ~19 tok/s on 7B Q4_K_M single-stream in llama.cpp — at or above your 20 tok/s target — because token generation is memory-bandwidth-bound (192.2 GB/s) and the sm_61 INT8 dp4a/MMQ path is the fast path that llama.cpp already uses. The real levers are (1) picking the fastest-fitting quant, (2) keeping context short, and (3) treating the 9 cards as a parallel candidate-farm, not a single fast pipeline.
- **Do NOT spread one model across cards to go faster.** Over PCIe 1.0 x1, tensor-parallel dies and layer-parallel only adds VRAM capacity while one card idles — it does not raise single-stream tok/s. The architecture-correct play is 9 independent replicas each emitting ~20–30 tok/s, fanning out best-of-N candidates verified by your existing Credence auto-fixer + execution-feedback loop.
- **The highest-leverage speed levers, ranked:** (1) confirm/tune the per-card AR baseline (Q4_0/Q5_K_M, short ctx, FORCE_MMQ) — near-certain, days of work; (2) prompt-lookup / n-gram speculative decoding for templated OTP boilerplate — free, no draft model, code-specific evidence of large gains; (3) candidate-farm across 9 cards for wall-clock-to-first-passing-program. Avoid diffusion (CPU-bound in llama.cpp today) and single-card draft-model speculation (won't fit 7B+draft in 6GB and Pascal lacks the cheap-verify advantage).

## Key Findings

**1. The bottleneck you fear is already solved at the per-card level.** Token generation (tg) is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. Per the TechPowerUp GPU Database (EVGA/Palit/Manli P106-100 entries), the card has **192.2 GB/s GDDR5 bandwidth** (192-bit bus, 2002 MHz / 8 Gbps effective, GP106-100-A1 die, 1280 CUDA cores, Pascal/sm_61, PCIe 1.0 x16, released Jun 19, 2017). Measured llama.cpp `llama-bench` results from GitHub Discussion #15013 (user pebaryan, Aug 2025): **P106-100 = 30.40 tok/s tg128 on Llama-2-7B Q4_0** (30.64 with FA); the GTX 1060 6GB twin = 27.79 tok/s. LocalScore's GTX 1060 6GB database shows **19.1 tok/s on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B Q4_K_M, 62.5 tok/s on a 1.5B, and 76.9 tok/s on Llama-3.2-1B** (all Q4_K_M). These are real single-stream numbers and they clear or approach your 20 tok/s bar without any exotic technique.

**2. Flash Attention's absence barely matters for your workload.** FA mostly accelerates long-context prefill and reduces KV memory; at the short contexts code generation uses, the measured P106-100 delta between FA off and on is tiny (30.40 → 30.64 tok/s). The lack of an FA MMA path on sm_61 is therefore close to irrelevant for tg at code-gen context lengths. It will only bite on very long prompts (large repo context), which you can mitigate with retrieval rather than huge prompts.

**3. Quant choice is a speed knob, and the Pascal fast path is INT8 dp4a/MMQ.** Because tg is bandwidth-bound, fewer bytes-per-weight = more tok/s. Q4_0 is the simplest dequant and is consistently the fastest legacy quant on Pascal; K-quants (Q4_K_M) are slightly heavier to compute but still bandwidth-dominated; IQ-quants (codebook lookups) are known to be slow on hardware without fast lookup, so avoid IQ2/IQ3 for *speed* on Pascal. llama.cpp automatically selects the MMQ/dp4a INT8 path on sm_61 (which lacks INT8 tensor cores but has dp4a), and this is the good fast path your prior research already identified.

**4. Multi-card over PCIe 1.0 x1 does NOT speed up a single stream.** llama.cpp's official multi-GPU docs state that `--split-mode layer` (pipeline parallelism, the default and most compatible) runs layers sequentially across GPUs — one card works while the others idle — and explicitly "can tolerate slow interconnect speeds." `--split-mode row`/tensor parallelism needs an all-reduce every layer and is "much more bottlenecked by GPU interconnect speed," which is fatal on x1 risers. The practical consequence: for a model that already fits in one 6GB card, splitting it across cards adds latency, not throughput. The KnightLi multi-GPU benchmark confirms a split model is typically "10% to 40% slower" than one card that holds it. **Multi-card only helps aggregate throughput of many independent streams, or enables models too big for one card.**

**5. Speculative decoding is real in llama.cpp but the math is hostile on a single 6GB Pascal card.** llama.cpp supports draft-model speculation (`--model-draft`/`-md`), n-gram self-speculation (`--spec-type ngram-simple/ngram-mod`), and prompt-lookup. Measured code-gen speedups on a fast GPU (RTX 4070, Discussion #10466): Qwen2.5-Coder 0.5B drafting a 14B target hits **2.5× on coding** at 10 draft tokens; 1.5B draft = 1.63×; general text only 1.4×. BUT: (a) a 7B target + draft model won't both fit in 6GB; (b) speculative decoding only wins when the target verify-step is cheap relative to draft, and on bandwidth-bound Pascal each verify pass still reads the full model from VRAM, so the win shrinks; (c) Pascal-specific measured speculative numbers do not exist in public benchmarks — this is a genuine evidence gap. Net: draft-model speculation is a poor fit for a single P106; n-gram/prompt-lookup is the version worth probing because it costs zero extra VRAM.

**6. Prompt-lookup / n-gram speculative decoding is the controversial high-value probe for templated Elixir/OTP.** Prompt Lookup Decoding and llama.cpp's n-gram modes draft tokens by matching the recent context against the prompt/history — no draft model, no extra VRAM. The arXiv study "Speculative Decoding: Performance or Illusion?" (Liu, Yu, Park, Stoica & Cheung, **arXiv:2601.11580**, Dec 30 2025; measured on H100/vLLM) finds that once **BLEU-4 prompt-output overlap exceeds ~0.6, n-gram speculation "consistently outperforms EAGLE/EAGLE-3 across all batch sizes, achieving up to 100% higher speedup"** on code-editing tasks; and that adaptively combining n-gram + EAGLE reaches an oracle **4.9× speedup** on InstructCoder (vs ~2.2× for the best single method) — because **verification "dominates execution time, ranging from ~42% to ~95%."** OTP/GenServer code is exactly this kind of high-overlap, templated text. The honest counter-evidence: a DEV.to home-cluster test found n-gram gave a deceptive 5× only on *repeated identical prompts* and little gain on 8 diverse prompts; and the Qwen3.6-35B-A3B RTX-3090 benchmark found *net-negative* speculation on an MoE target even at 100% acceptance (expert-loading overhead). So the gain is real but workload-specific — it must be measured on *your* Elixir prompts.

**7. Diffusion LLMs do not (yet) help you on Pascal.** Dream-7B and LLaDA are merged in llama.cpp (`llama-diffusion-cli`), and DiffuCoder-7B shares Dream's architecture. But the llama.cpp implementation samples on CPU and is explicitly "slow," "CPU-bound," and for "educational purposes" — in the merge PR (am17an, #14644), 87% of step time is logits CPU↔GPU transfer. Published diffusion throughput wins are all measured on H100/A100 with FP16 and custom kernels: **Fast-dLLM (NVIDIA, arXiv:2505.22618) reports "up to 27.6× throughput" but specifically on GSM8K 8-shot at generation length 1024 (LLaDA reaching 19.3 tok/s)** — its *code-specific* gain (HumanEval, length 512) is only ~2.9×; **Streaming-dLLM (arXiv:2601.17917) reports HumanEval 0-shot LLaDA speedups of ~5.5× at gen-length 256 and up to ~14.4× at gen-length 512.** None of these survive sm_61's FP16-at-1/64 penalty and no-FA limits. Combined with your prior finding that diffusion adds no Elixir capability and fails multi-function specs, diffusion is not a near-term speed play on this hardware. The only defensible diffusion idea is a *future* diffusion-drafter feeding an AR verifier, but it's blocked by the CPU-bound engine today.

**8. Small-active-param MoE is fast but VRAM-hungry, and the speculation interaction is bad.** A 30–35B MoE with ~3B active params (Qwen3-30B-A3B class) reads only active-expert bytes per token, so per-token compute is ~3B-model-cheap — but all experts must be resident, needing ~20GB, far over 6GB. With `-ncmoe` expert-offload to CPU RAM the experts cross PCIe 1.0 x1 per token, which would be ruinous on your interconnect. MoE also breaks speculation: the Qwen3.6-35B-A3B benchmark showed speculation is net-negative on MoE because each drafted token pulls fresh expert slices. Verdict: small dense models (0.5–7B) are the right family for this rig, not MoE.

**9. Mamba/RWKV (state-space/linear-attention) run in llama.cpp and are worth a capability+speed probe.** llama.cpp supports RWKV-6/7 and Mamba/Mamba-2 with recurrent state (no growing KV cache). RWKV-7-World comes in 1.5B/2.9B GGUF. These have flat memory with context (advantage for long repo context) and small sizes that fit 6GB easily. They are still bandwidth-bound for weights, so a 2.9B SSM ≈ a 2.9B transformer in tok/s, but the lack of KV-cache growth helps at long context. Their Elixir capability is unproven and likely weak — treat as a speculative capability experiment, not a primary path.

**10. The reframe that wins: candidate-farm + execution-feedback = high *usable* tok/s even if each stream is slow.** Test-time-scaling literature shows parallel best-of-N sampling improves pass@k with N at low latency because samples run concurrently. Snell, Lee, Xu & Kumar (**arXiv:2408.03314**, Aug 2024) show compute-optimal test-time scaling is **">4× more efficient than best-of-N"** and that "on problems where a smaller base model attains somewhat non-trivial success rates, test-time compute can be used to outperform a **14× larger model**" (FLOPs-matched). Your 9 cards each running an independent replica at ~20–30 tok/s give an aggregate of ~180–270 tok/s spread across 9 candidates. With your Credence auto-fixer + test verification selecting the first passing program, the user-facing metric becomes **wall-clock-to-first-passing-program**, not single-stream tok/s — and 9 slow-but-correct parallel candidates can beat 1 fast GPU for this verify-heavy workload.

## Details

### The memory-bandwidth tok/s ceiling table (P106-100, 192.2 GB/s)
*Reasoning (clearly labeled):* tg ceiling ≈ bandwidth ÷ bytes-read-per-token ≈ 192.2 GB/s ÷ model-size-in-VRAM. Real efficiency on Pascal is ~55–70% of ceiling (measured 7B Q4_0: 30.4 vs ~50.6 ceiling ≈ 60%). VRAM must also hold KV cache + context; 6GB caps you around 7B at Q4–Q5.

| Model / quant | Approx VRAM | Theoretical tg ceiling | Expected real tg (≈60%) | Measured (source) | Fits 6GB? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1B Q4_K_M | ~0.8 GB | ~240 t/s | ~140 t/s | 76.9 (LocalScore, Llama-3.2-1B) | Yes |
| 1.5B Q4_K_M | ~1.0 GB | ~190 t/s | ~115 t/s | 62.5 (LocalScore, DS-R1-1.5B) | Yes |

| 7B Q4_0 | ~3.8 GB | ~50 t/s | ~30 t/s | **30.4 (GH #15013, P106-100)** | Yes |
| 7B Q4_K_M | ~4.4 GB | ~44 t/s | ~26 t/s | 19.1 (LocalScore, real mix) | Yes |
| 7B Q5_K_M | ~4.8 GB | ~40 t/s | ~24 t/s | — | Tight |
| 7B Q6_K | ~5.5 GB | ~35 t/s | ~21 t/s | — | Very tight |
| 7B Q8_0 | ~7.2 GB | ~27 t/s | ~16 t/s | — | No (OOM) |

**Implication:** every quant that fits 6GB at 7B clears or approaches 20 tok/s in expectation. A fine-tuned 1.5B Elixir model runs at 60+ tok/s — 3× your target — so your MultiPL-T-style fine-tune is "free" on speed and only constrained on the multi-function ceiling.

### Build flags that actually matter on sm_61
- Build with CUDA 12.x and `CMAKE_CUDA_ARCHITECTURES=61-real` (you have this). llama.cpp even suggests `61-virtual` + `GGML_CUDA_FORCE_MMQ` to push the Pascal INT8 path onto newer cards, confirming MMQ is *the* Pascal kernel.
- `GGML_CUDA_FORCE_MMQ=ON`: forces the quantized-matmul MMQ kernels. On sm_61 (no INT8 tensor cores) llama.cpp already routes to MMQ/dp4a, but forcing it avoids any cuBLAS fallback that upcasts to FP16 (catastrophic at 1/64 FP16 rate). A P40 user (Issue #6814) saw a 62% tg collapse from a kernel-selection regression — proof that kernel path dominates Pascal speed.
- `LLAMA_CUDA_FORCE_DMMV=1`: an active P40 multi-card user (Discussion #19248) runs this in production getting 20+ tok/s — worth A/B testing on P106.
- KV cache: use F16 KV (default). Avoid pushing KV to FP16-compute paths. Keep context small (2–4k) for code-gen.
- `-fa` (Flash Attention): negligible tg effect at short context on Pascal; leave default. CUDA graphs are gated off below Ampere — expected, don't fight it.
- `-b`/`-ub` (batch/ubatch): only matters for prompt processing and batched/parallel serving, not single-stream tg.

### Path-by-path verdict with local probes

**PATH 1 — Per-card AR (VERIFIED, primary path).** Runs on sm_61: yes (this is the measured baseline). Effect: 20–30 tok/s at 7B, 60+ at 1.5B. VRAM: 4–5GB at 7B Q4. *Probe (1 day):* `llama-bench -m {7B}.Q4_0.gguf -m {7B}.Q4_K_M.gguf -m {7B}.Q5_K_M.gguf -ngl 99 -p 512 -n 128` on one P106; pick the fastest quant that holds your Elixir quality. A/B `GGML_CUDA_FORCE_MMQ` and `LLAMA_CUDA_FORCE_DMMV`.

**PATH 2 — Speculation.** Draft-model: runs on sm_61 but won't fit 7B+draft in 6GB; skip on single card. Prompt-lookup/n-gram: runs on sm_61, zero extra VRAM, code-specific evidence of large gains. *Probe (2–3 days):* `llama-cli -m {7B}.gguf --spec-type ngram-mod --draft-max 16` on 20 real Elixir/OTP generation prompts; read the printed `draft acceptance rate` and tok/s. Hypothesis to falsify: templated GenServer boilerplate gives BLEU-4 overlap >0.6 and >1.5× tg. *Controversial variant:* seed a static n-gram cache (`common_ngram_cache_load`) with your most common OTP skeletons (supervisor trees, GenServer callbacks) so the very first generation benefits.

**PATH 3 — Multi-card.** Tensor-parallel: dies on x1 (verified by docs). Pipeline/layer: adds VRAM, not single-stream speed (verified). *Probe (1 day):* run the SAME 7B model on 1 card vs `-sm layer` across 3 cards with `llama-bench`; expect equal-or-worse tg, confirming you should NOT split. Then pivot to the candidate-farm (Path 6).

**PATH 4 — Diffusion.** Dream/LLaDA run via `llama-diffusion-cli` but are CPU-bound and slow today; no Pascal speed win. *Probe (0.5 day, falsification):* time `llama-diffusion-cli -m dream7b-q4.gguf --diffusion-steps 128 -ngl 99` on one P106 vs AR 7B tok/s. Expect diffusion to LOSE on Pascal. Park unless llama.cpp adds GPU sampling.

**PATH 5 — Smaller/faster & alternatives.** Sub-1.5B: 60+ tok/s, carries single-function Elixir (your fine-tune). MoE: doesn't fit / x1-hostile / breaks speculation — skip. Mamba/RWKV: run in llama.cpp, flat KV, fit easily — capability-unproven probe. Marlin/GPTQ: Marlin kernels are sm_80+ only (vLLM docs); vLLM has no real sm_61 fast path and its GGUF path is slow — stay on llama.cpp. *Probe:* benchmark your fine-tuned 1.5B for tok/s AND run it as the generator in the candidate-farm.

**PATH 6 — Reframe (the meta-win).** *Probe (3–5 days):* stand up `llama-server` replicas pinned one-per-card (`CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES`), fan out N candidate generations for one Elixir spec, pipe each through Credence + your execution-feedback repair loop, return first passing. Measure wall-clock-to-first-passing-program at N=1 vs N=9. This is where 9 slow cards beat 1 fast GPU.

### Generate-less strategies (orthogonal speed multiplier)
The real metric is *useful-tokens-generated-per-program*. Constrained/skeleton decoding (emit a compact AST/skeleton, deterministically expand), grammar-constrained decoding to Elixir's grammar (llama.cpp GBNF), and retrieval of whole boilerplate blocks all reduce novel tokens generated — directly multiplying effective tok/s regardless of raw decode speed. This pairs naturally with the n-gram cache (Path 2) since retrieved blocks become high-acceptance drafts, and it directly exploits the finding that *verification dominates* speculative execution cost — fewer novel tokens means fewer expensive verify passes.

## Recommendations

**Single highest-leverage experiment to run FIRST:** the Stage 0 `llama-bench` quant sweep on one P106-100. It is one command, takes under an hour, and will almost certainly show you are already at or above 20 tok/s — collapsing the entire "throughput crisis" and redirecting all effort to quality + the candidate-farm.

**Stage 0 — Lock the baseline (this week).** Run the Path 1 quant sweep. Decision threshold: pick the heaviest quant that holds ≥20 tok/s tg *and* your Elixir quality bar — likely Q4_0 or Q5_K_M at 7B, or your fine-tuned 1.5B at 60+ tok/s. Verify `GGML_CUDA_FORCE_MMQ`/`LLAMA_CUDA_FORCE_DMMV` don't regress. **If baseline already ≥20 tok/s (expected), the throughput problem is solved and you shift entirely to quality + candidate-farm.**

**Stage 1 — Free speed from n-gram speculation (next).** Enable `--spec-type ngram-mod` and measure acceptance on real OTP prompts. Keep it if net tg gain >1.3× and quality is unchanged (speculation is lossless by construction); drop it if acceptance <40% or tg regresses (as seen on MoE/UMA cases). Threshold to escalate to a static OTP-skeleton n-gram cache: acceptance 40–60% on boilerplate-heavy prompts.

**Stage 2 — Candidate-farm across 9 cards.** Deploy 9 single-card replicas, fan out best-of-N, verify with Credence + tests, return first pass. Benchmark wall-clock-to-first-passing-program. This is the structural answer to "usable tok/s" and the regime where your hardware shines.

**Stage 3 — Capability probes (parallel, lower priority).** Test RWKV-7-2.9B and your fine-tuned 1.5B for multi-function Elixir. Run the diffusion falsification probe once to close that door with data.

**Ranked shortlist of concrete local probes:**
1. `llama-bench` 7B quant sweep (Q4_0/Q4_K_M/Q5_K_M) + FORCE_MMQ/DMMV A/B — one card, <1 day. *Falsifies "we're below 20 tok/s."*
2. `--spec-type ngram-mod --draft-max 16` on 20 real Elixir/OTP prompts; read acceptance rate + tok/s — 2–3 days. *Tests the templated-boilerplate speculation hypothesis.*
3. Candidate-farm: 9 one-per-card `llama-server` replicas + best-of-N + Credence/test verify; measure wall-clock-to-first-passing-program at N=1 vs N=9 — 3–5 days. *Tests the reframe.*
4. 1-card vs 3-card `-sm layer` `llama-bench` on the same 7B — 1 day. *Confirms multi-card does NOT help single-stream (don't split).*
5. Diffusion falsification: `llama-diffusion-cli` Dream-7B-Q4 tok/s vs AR 7B on one P106 — 0.5 day. *Closes the diffusion door with local data.*

**What would change the plan:** if measured baseline tg <15 tok/s at acceptable quality, drop to a smaller/fine-tuned model (cheap, 60+ tok/s). If n-gram acceptance is high (>60%) on your prompts, invest in the static OTP n-gram cache and skeleton decoding. If llama.cpp ships GPU-side diffusion sampling, re-evaluate Dream/DiffuCoder as a drafter.

## Caveats
- **Measured-vs-reasoned:** The 30.4 tok/s (7B Q4_0) and 19.1/62.5/76.9 (Q4_K_M) numbers are *measured* (GitHub #15013 user pebaryan; LocalScore GTX 1060 6GB). The ceiling-table efficiencies and the 3B row are *reasoning/interpolation* — no measured 3B P106 datapoint exists. Run your own `llama-bench` to confirm.
- **Evidence gaps flagged by research:** (1) no clean measured P106-100 multi-card single-stream split delta over x1 risers (the one dual-P106 blog datapoint, 3.08→6.36 t/s, was a 13B model spilling out of 6GB — capacity, not parallel speedup); (2) no Pascal-specific speculative-decoding measured speedup — all spec-decode numbers are Ada/Ampere/Apple. Treat speculation gains as "probable for code, must measure locally."
- **Speculation is workload-dependent and can be net-negative** (MoE expert-loading; UMA serialization; diverse-prompt cases). The code-gain evidence (arXiv:2601.11580) is strong but conditional on high prompt-output overlap (BLEU-4 >0.6).
- **Diffusion throughput claims (up to 27.6×, 14.4×) are H100/A100 FP16 results** at long generation lengths and do not transfer to sm_61; code-specific gains are smaller (~2.9× HumanEval for Fast-dLLM) and the llama.cpp diffusion path is CPU-bound today.
- **Quant speed ordering on Pascal** (Q4_0 fastest, IQ slow) is well-supported by general llama.cpp evidence but should be confirmed on your exact build/cards.
- Some throughput blog figures cited for context (daily.dev, sitepoint, KnightLi) are vendor/aggregator-adjacent; the load-bearing numbers here are from llama.cpp GitHub, LocalScore, arXiv (2601.11580, 2505.22618, 2601.17917, 2408.03314), TechPowerUp, and official llama.cpp docs.
